Outcomes Definition & Theory of Change

How clearly are your outcomes defined? Vague outcomes make evidence impossible to collect or assess.

Why this matters: The Nesta Standards of Evidence begin at Level 1 with "you can describe what you do and why it matters." Many organisations skip this step and jump to data collection — resulting in data that doesn't prove anything useful.
Outcome Clarity
Rate each aspect of how you've defined your intended outcomes
Specificity of outcomes
Are outcomes specific, measurable, and time-bound? (e.g. "20% increase in household income within 18 months" not "improve livelihoods")
Theory of Change quality
Do you have a documented ToC showing inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impact, with explicit assumptions?
Stakeholder involvement in outcome definition
Were beneficiaries and affected communities involved in defining what "success" means?
Outcome vs. output clarity
Are you clearly distinguishing outputs (things you produce) from outcomes (changes in beneficiaries) from impact (long-term societal change)?

Data Collection Quality

Rate the quality of how you collect data on the outcomes you've defined. Data quality directly determines what claims you can make.

Data Collection Assessment
Be honest — funders and investors are increasingly able to spot data quality issues
Sample size & representativeness
Is your sample large enough to be statistically meaningful? Is it representative of your target population (not just the easiest-to-reach)?
Data collection method rigour
Self-reported surveys are less reliable than validated instruments; administrative records are better than recall. What are you using?
Baseline data availability
Without a baseline (pre-intervention measurement), you cannot show change — only current status. This is one of the most common evidence gaps.
Data independence
Is data collected and analysed by people with an interest in positive outcomes? Self-assessment is inherently biased; independent verification matters.
Data frequency & consistency
One-off data points are weak. Consistent longitudinal data enables trend analysis and demonstrates sustained impact.

Attribution, Causality & Counterfactual

The hardest — and most important — question: would this change have happened anyway, without your intervention?

⚠️ The Attribution Challenge

DFID's "How to Note on Attribution" (2012) identifies attribution as the single biggest weakness in development sector evidence. Showing that beneficiaries improved after your programme is not the same as showing they improved because of your programme. Investors and DFIs are now requiring counterfactual analysis for any evidence-based claims.

DFID How to Note: Attribution (2012) J-PAL Counterfactual Methods 3ie Evidence Framework
Attribution & Counterfactual Assessment
Counterfactual analysis
How do you estimate what would have happened without your programme? (Deadweight, SROI term). Options range from assumption-based estimates to RCTs.
Attribution of change to your programme
What % of observed change do you attribute to your programme vs other factors? Have you applied contribution analysis or other attribution methods?
Selection bias management
Are the people who receive your programme systematically different from those who don't (self-selection, cherry-picking, referral bias)? How is this addressed?
Unintended consequences documented
Do you document negative outcomes, trade-offs, or unexpected effects? Credible evidence includes honest reporting of what didn't work or who was harmed.

Comparators, Benchmarks & Value for Money

Funders and investors increasingly ask: "Is this a good use of money compared to alternatives?" Having benchmarks makes your evidence dramatically more compelling.

Benchmarking & Comparability
External benchmarks for outcomes
Can you compare your outcomes to sector averages, similar programmes, or published benchmarks? (e.g. your cost-per-job vs DFID's $1,000 benchmark)
Cost-effectiveness analysis
Do you know your cost per outcome (e.g. cost per life year saved, cost per child educated, cost per job created)? Can you defend this to a funder?
Value for Money narrative
Can you articulate why your approach represents good value vs alternatives (including doing nothing)? UK FCDO and many impact investors require VfM analysis.
Durability of outcomes (drop-off)
Do you track outcomes beyond programme end? Drop-off analysis asks: how much impact remains after 1, 2, 5 years? Required for SROI and most DFI evidence.

M&E Systems, Processes & Culture

The best evidence strategy fails without the right systems and organisational culture to support it.

Organisational M&E Capacity
Dedicated M&E capacity
Is impact measurement someone's primary job, or a bolt-on to programme roles? Dedicated M&E staff produce significantly better quality evidence.
Data management systems
Is data stored in Excel spreadsheets (high risk), a purpose-built system (better), or a research-grade database with version control?
Learning loop — evidence to decision
Does your evidence actually change how you operate? Or does it sit in reports nobody reads? Bond calls this "evidence-informed decision making."
External reporting & transparency
Do you publish your evidence publicly, including limitations and failures? Transparency is increasingly required by funders and builds long-term credibility.

📊 NESTA Evidence Ladder

5
Independent replication
RCT replicated in multiple contexts; systematic review. Required by J-PAL, major DFIs.
4
Independent evaluation
Controlled comparison (quasi-experimental). Used by Gates Foundation, USAID.
3
Correlational evidence
Pre/post with comparison group. Standard for most impact investors.
2
Pre/post measurement
Baseline + endline without control. Minimum for grant reporting.
1
Logic model
Theory of change + output tracking. Minimum viable level.

→ Part-time M&E officer

→ Full longitudinal study
→ Randomisation where ethical
→ Academic partnership for credibility
