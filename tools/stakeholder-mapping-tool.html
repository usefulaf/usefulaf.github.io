Power-Interest Matrix (Mendelow, 1991)

Framework: Mendelow's matrix classifies stakeholders into four quadrants based on their power to influence and interest in your project. Originally published by Aubrey Mendelow at the Strategic Management Society Conference (1991). It remains the most widely used stakeholder prioritisation tool in development practice, cited in DFID (now FCDO) and World Bank guidance.

Mitchell-Agle-Wood Salience Model (1997)

Framework: Mitchell, Agle & Wood (1997) "Toward a Theory of Stakeholder Identification and Salience" in Academy of Management Review 22(4), 853-886. This model classifies stakeholders based on three attributes — Power, Legitimacy, and Urgency — into seven categories of decreasing salience. Stakeholders with all three attributes are "definitive" and demand immediate management attention.

Stakeholder Engagement Plan

Based on: AA1000 Stakeholder Engagement Standard (AccountAbility, 2015 revision). The AA1000 SES defines five engagement levels — Inform, Consult, Involve, Collaborate, Empower — each with specific methods, resource requirements, and expected outcomes. The recommended level is derived from each stakeholder's matrix quadrant and salience classification. The World Bank's 2018 Environmental & Social Framework (ESS10) uses a compatible engagement hierarchy.

Risk & Conflict Analysis

Why this matters: DFID (2003) "Tools for Development" identifies stakeholder conflicts as a top-5 project risk. Mapping potential conflicts between stakeholders — and between stakeholders and the project — helps design mitigation strategies before crises emerge. The IFC Performance Standard 1 (PS1) requires stakeholder risk assessment for all Category A and B projects.
