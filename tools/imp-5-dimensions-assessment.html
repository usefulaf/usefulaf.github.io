🏢 Organisation & Initiative Context
Establish the scope of this IMP assessment. This information anchors all five dimension analyses.
💡 About the IMP Framework: The Impact Management Project (IMP) was convened in 2016 and engaged 2,000+ impact practitioners to establish a consensus framework. It defines five dimensions that all impactful activities share: WHO is affected, WHAT outcomes occur, HOW MUCH change happens, the CONTRIBUTION this represents, and RISK of the outcomes not occurring as expected. The framework is endorsed by GIIN, GRI, SASB, OECD and used by major DFIs including IFC, CDC, FMO and DEG.
👥

WHO experiences the outcome?

Identify the people (or planet) affected by your activities, their level of underservice, and the depth of your relationship with them. IMP distinguishes between primary stakeholders (direct beneficiaries), secondary stakeholders (indirect), and the natural environment.

IMP Dimension 1 | IRIS+ Categories: Stakeholder Engagement, Beneficiary Characteristics
Consider households, communities, workers in supply chains, local ecosystems, future generations.
🎯

WHAT outcomes occur for stakeholders?

Specify the outcomes experienced by stakeholders — the changes in their lives or wellbeing — distinguishing between outcomes (changes people experience) and outputs (activities you deliver). IMP classifies outcomes by importance to stakeholders and their position in the impact value chain.

IMP Dimension 2 | IRIS+ Categories: Product / Service Quality, Outcome Type
An outcome is a change in the condition, behaviour, attitude, knowledge or status of stakeholders — NOT an activity or output.
IMP Best Practice: Disclose potential negative outcomes even if the net impact is positive. Omitting this reduces assessment credibility.
📊

HOW MUCH change occurs?

Quantify the scale, depth, and duration of outcomes. IMP requires three sub-elements: (1) Scale — how many people experience the outcome; (2) Depth — how significant is the change for each person; and (3) Duration — how long does the change last. Source: IMP "How Much" guidance, GIIN IRIS+ core metrics.

IMP Dimension 3 | Sub-elements: Scale, Depth, Duration
📏 Scale — How many stakeholders experience the outcome?
Number of people (or hectares/tonnes for environmental) currently experiencing the outcome.
🔬 Depth — How significant is the change?
Specify the unit of measurement. IMP recommends measuring change from baseline rather than absolute end-state.
⏱ Duration — How long does the change last?
⚡

What is your CONTRIBUTION to the outcome?

Assess what would have happened anyway (counterfactual), and what your specific role is in causing the outcome. IMP's contribution analysis draws on the OECD DAC criterion of "additionality" and distinguishes between direct causation, facilitation, and indirect influence. This dimension requires the most honest self-assessment.

IMP Dimension 4 | Related: OECD DAC Additionality, SROI Deadweight & Attribution
⚠️

What is the RISK to the impact?

Assess the likelihood and consequences of outcomes not materialising as expected. IMP identifies six types of impact risk that apply across all initiatives. Disclosing risk does not weaken your case — it demonstrates credibility. Source: IMP "A Guide to Classifying the Impact of an Investment" (2019), adapted from IRIS+ risk taxonomy.

IMP Dimension 5 | 6 Impact Risk Types | Source: IMP (2019)
📋 Rate each of the 6 IMP impact risk types below. For each, assess: How likely is this risk to materialise? (High/Medium/Low) and what mitigation is in place?
1️⃣ Evidence Risk — Our evidence for the impact claim may be insufficient
The outcomes claimed may not actually occur or may be weaker than assumed. Evidence may be too thin to support the impact narrative. Source: IMP Risk Type 1.
2️⃣ Execution Risk — Activities may not be delivered as planned
Even if the theory of change is correct, operational failures (staffing, technology, logistics, partners) may prevent outcomes. Source: IMP Risk Type 2.
3️⃣ Alignment Risk — Stakeholder expectations may not match what we deliver
The outcomes we claim to deliver may not match what stakeholders actually want or need. Our theory of change may be misaligned with beneficiary priorities. Source: IMP Risk Type 3.
4️⃣ Efficiency Risk — Better alternatives may exist for producing these outcomes
Another organisation or approach may produce the same or better outcomes at lower cost or risk. Resources may not be optimally allocated. Source: IMP Risk Type 4.
5️⃣ Endurance Risk — Positive outcomes may not last
Outcomes may fade after your programme ends. Dependency created rather than sustainable change. Drop-off rate may be higher than assumed. Source: IMP Risk Type 5. SROI Standard requires drop-off % per year.
6️⃣ Unexpected Impact Risk — Negative unintended consequences may occur
Actions may cause harm to stakeholders, the environment, or third parties that wasn't anticipated. Displacement effects, market distortions, cultural harm, or exacerbating inequality. Source: IMP Risk Type 6.
