🔐 Mission Lock & Legal Structure Selector

Choose the right legal structure for your social enterprise or impact organisation — and understand what really protects your mission. Based on actual legal requirements for B Corp, CIC, Benefit Corporation, NPO, and hybrid structures across UK, US, and global contexts.

How this works: Answer 8 questions about your organisation's priorities. The tool will recommend the best-fit legal structure and explain why — based on mission protection strength, funder eligibility, governance requirements, and tax treatment. Sources: UK Companies House / Charity Commission guidance; US IRS 501(c)(3) rules; B Lab Global certification standards; IFC / GIIN blended finance frameworks.
1 Where is your organisation primarily operating?
This determines which legal structures are available to you.
2 What is your primary revenue model?
How you make money affects which structures are viable and how tax treatment works.
3 How important is protecting the mission if the organisation is sold or leadership changes?
Mission drift risk varies enormously by structure — some structures have legally enforceable mission lock, others rely purely on culture and governance.
4 Do you want to raise equity investment (give shareholders ownership stakes)?
Charities and most NPOs cannot issue equity. CICs and Benefit Corps can, with restrictions.
5 How important is brand credibility with mainstream funders (government, foundations, DFIs)?
Some legal forms are more recognisable and trusted by traditional funders. This affects your fundraising options.
6 What is your team's capacity for governance and compliance?
Different structures have very different regulatory burdens. Charity regulation in the UK is notoriously complex.
7 What stage is your organisation?
Early-stage organisations often benefit from simpler structures they can upgrade later.
8 Is profit distribution to shareholders/founders important?
Charities and most NPOs cannot distribute profits. CICs have dividend caps. Benefit Corps and regular LLCs/Ltd companies can distribute freely.

🇬🇧 UK Charity (Charitable Incorporated Organisation or Company Limited by Guarantee)

Strong Mission Lock Tax Exempt No Equity

How it works

Registered with the Charity Commission (England & Wales), OSCR (Scotland), or CCNI (Northern Ireland). Must have exclusively charitable objects (poverty, education, religion, health, arts, environment, etc.). The CIO structure combines legal personality with simplified governance — recommended over CLC for most new charities.

Mission protection mechanisms

  • Charitable objects locked in constitution — can only be changed with Charity Commission approval
  • Trustees cannot benefit personally (no salary unless authorised)
  • Assets must be applied for charitable purposes only, including on dissolution
  • Charity Commission can appoint interim managers if governance fails

Tax benefits

  • Exemption from Income/Corporation Tax on most income
  • Gift Aid: donors reclaim 25p per £1 donated
  • 80% mandatory business rates relief (100% discretionary)
  • Capital Gains Tax exemption on asset disposals
💡 Best use: Delivering public benefit services primarily funded by grants, donations, and contracts. The gold standard for government and foundation funders.
⚠️ Watch out: Trustee liability, complex annual reporting to Charity Commission, restrictions on trading (must be ancillary or through a trading subsidiary). If you want equity investment, a charity structure will block you.

🇬🇧 Community Interest Company (CIC)

Asset Lock Limited Dividends UK-Only

How it works

Introduced under the Companies (Audit, Investigations and Community Enterprise) Act 2004. Regulated by the CIC Regulator. Must pass a Community Interest Test (CIT) on registration. Comes in two forms: CIC Limited by Guarantee (no shares, best for grants) or CIC Limited by Shares (can issue equity, take social investment).

Dividend cap rules (CIC Regulations 2005)

  • Maximum dividend per share: 35% of distributable profit
  • Interest on loans: capped at base rate + 4%
  • These caps are set by the CIC Regulator, not negotiable
  • Asset lock prevents assets being transferred below market value

Annual reporting

Annual Community Interest Report required, filed at Companies House. Must demonstrate ongoing community benefit. CIC Regulator can investigate complaints and, in extremis, wind up the CIC.

💡 Best use: UK social enterprises that want to trade commercially, take social investment or limited equity, and signal community purpose without full charity regulation burden.
⚠️ Watch out: No Gift Aid. Not eligible for most charitable foundation grants. Dividend cap can deter commercial investors. Recognition is lower than charity or B Corp internationally.

🇺🇸 US 501(c)(3) Nonprofit

Tax Exempt Donor Deductions No Equity

How it works

Federal tax exemption under Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code. Must be organised and operated exclusively for charitable, educational, religious, or scientific purposes. Two main types: Public Charity (receives broad public support) vs Private Foundation (funded by one source). Most social enterprises should aim for Public Charity status.

Key compliance requirements

  • Annual Form 990 filing (publicly available — full transparency)
  • No private inurement (no profit to insiders)
  • Limited lobbying; no political campaign activity
  • Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) applies to non-mission trading

Mission protection

Articles of Incorporation lock in charitable purpose. On dissolution, remaining assets must go to another 501(c)(3). State Attorney General has oversight of charitable assets. IRS can revoke tax-exempt status for violations.

💡 Best use: US-based impact organisations primarily funded by foundations, government grants, or individual donors. The standard structure for most US nonprofits and NGOs.
⚠️ Watch out: Form 990 is public — funders, journalists, and competitors can see your financials. State AG oversight is active in some states (NY, CA). Earned income at scale requires careful UBIT planning.

🇺🇸 Benefit Corporation / Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

Stakeholder Duties Equity OK Varies by State

How it works

Available in 39+ US states (as of 2024). Directors are legally required to consider all stakeholders (employees, community, environment) — not just shareholders. Delaware PBC (used by Patagonia, Kickstarter, Allbirds) is the gold standard. Annual benefit report required.

Director liability shield

The key innovation: directors cannot be sued for prioritising social/environmental interests over shareholder returns, as long as they consider all stakeholders. This removes the legal risk of traditional "shareholder primacy" doctrine.

Mission protection limitations

  • No asset lock — company can be sold to non-mission buyer
  • Benefit purpose can be changed by supermajority vote (usually 2/3)
  • Annual benefit report required but third-party verification is not mandatory (unlike B Corp certification)
💡 Best use: US impact businesses seeking venture capital or equity investors while wanting legal protection for mission consideration. Particularly strong in Delaware due to established case law.
⚠️ Watch out: Benefit Corp ≠ B Corp. Benefit Corp is a legal structure; B Corp is a certification. You can be one without the other. Some impact investors specifically require B Corp certification, not just Benefit Corp status.

🌍 B Corp Certification

Certification (not legal structure) Global Recognition

How it works

B Corp is a certification from B Lab, not a legal structure. Any legal form (Ltd, LLC, CIC, Benefit Corp, etc.) can pursue B Corp certification. Requires: B Impact Assessment score of 80+ (out of 200); legal accountability amendment in governing documents; recertification every 3 years; public B Impact Report.

What it assesses (B Impact Assessment)

  • Governance (20 points max): transparency, ethics
  • Workers (40 points max): pay, benefits, ownership
  • Community (40 points max): local jobs, diversity, supply chain
  • Environment (20 points max): emissions, water, materials
  • Customers (customer-facing impact)

Notable B Corps (for credibility reference)

Patagonia, Danone (Europe), Ben & Jerry's, Innocent Drinks, The Guardian, Triodos Bank, Osprey, Natura. Over 7,500 certified globally (B Lab, 2024).

💡 Best use: Businesses that want globally recognised credibility with impact investors, large corporate partners, and B2B clients. Particularly powerful for supply chain or procurement relationships.
⚠️ Watch out: Cost: £1,000–£5,000+ in fees plus significant staff time for the BIA process. Certification can be lost. Some critics argue it has become easier to achieve since standards were revised in 2019. The legal amendment requirement varies by jurisdiction.

🌐 Hybrid Structure (Charity + Trading Subsidiary)

Best of Both High Complexity

How it works

A parent charity (CIO or CLG in UK; 501(c)(3) in US) owns a wholly-controlled trading subsidiary (Ltd company in UK; LLC or Inc. in US). The trading entity earns commercial income and gift-aids all profits to the parent charity. The charity retains grant eligibility and tax status while the subsidiary can trade freely.

Tax planning benefits

  • Trading subsidiary pays Corporation Tax; gifts profits to charity (Gift Aid)
  • Charity receives the gift tax-free — net tax position near zero
  • Charity retains Gift Aid on donations, business rates relief
  • Subsidiary can take equity investors if needed (in theory)

Governance requirements

Two separate boards (though trustees can overlap). Intercompany agreement/loan agreement needed. Annual accounts for both entities. Some duplication of overhead. Requires legal advice to set up properly.

💡 Best use: Established UK charities with significant trading income (>25% of turnover), or organisations wanting to access both charitable grants and commercial investment. Recommended annual income threshold: £500k+.
⚠️ Watch out: Two sets of accounts, two regulators (Charity Commission + Companies House), higher governance overhead, legal costs to establish. Not worth it below ~£300k income.
What actually protects mission? Legal structure is only one layer. The most durable mission protection combines legal lock with governance architecture and capital structure. Charities that fail their mission often do so through legal routes (gradual charitable objects creep, dominant trustees) rather than illegal ones. This matrix rates each protection mechanism by how robust it is in practice.

🏛️ Legal Protection Mechanisms

🔒
Statutory Asset LockAssets legally cannot leave the mission — applies to UK CICs and charities. The strongest form of protection. Survives leadership change, acquisition, and insolvency. (CIC Regulations 2005; Charities Act 2011)
📜
Locked Charitable Objects (UK Charity / US 501c3)Mission written into constitution; can only be changed with regulator approval. Very strong in practice — Charity Commission rarely approves mission changes that depart from original objects.
⚖️
Benefit Corporation Director Duties (US)Directors legally required to consider stakeholders. Moderate protection — does not prevent sale to a non-mission buyer, but shields directors who prioritise mission over short-term returns.
🏷️
B Corp Legal AmendmentB Lab requires an amendment to governing documents recognising stakeholder consideration. Moderate protection — enforcement relies on B Lab decertification, not courts.
📄
Mission Statement in Articles (Social Enterprise Ltd)Weak protection — can be changed by shareholder vote without regulator approval. Cultural signal only, not a legal barrier to mission drift.

🏗️ Governance Protection Mechanisms

👥
Independent Board MajorityIndependent trustees/directors who are not founders or investors, with genuine authority to challenge management. In practice the most important governance protection regardless of legal structure. NCVO Good Governance Code requires this for UK charities.
🗳️
Supermajority Requirements for Mission ChangeRequiring 75%+ or 2/3 vote to amend objects/purpose. Slows drift but does not prevent it if there is broad shareholder/trustee agreement. Benefit Corps typically require 2/3 shareholder vote to convert away from benefit purpose.
🔎
Impact Advisory Board / Stakeholder CouncilNon-binding advisory body representing mission stakeholders. Moderate protection — can surface concerns and create reputational pressure but has no legal authority.
📊
Impact Covenants in Investment AgreementsLegally binding impact obligations in shareholder agreements or loan covenants. Protection depends on how well-drafted the covenant is and who has enforcement rights. Standard in DFI and impact fund term sheets. (GIIN IRIS+ framework provides standard metrics for this)
✍️
Founder Veto Rights / Golden SharesWeak long-term protection — golden shares can be invalidated, founders leave, rights are diluted in subsequent investment rounds. Protects mission only as long as the founder is present and vigilant.

💰 Capital Structure Protection

🏦
Perpetual Endowment (Charity)Capital that can never be spent — only the income can be used. Locks both assets and mission in perpetuity. Only available to charities. Trustees need Charity Commission approval to spend endowment capital.
🌱
Patient Capital / Concessional Debt (No Equity Dilution)Growing through non-dilutive instruments (grants, loans, revenue-share) preserves founder control and avoids the shareholder primacy pressure that comes with equity investment.
📉
Impact-Linked Financial TermsLoan interest rates, equity returns, or bonus payments linked to impact KPIs. Creates financial incentive to maintain mission. Used in social impact bonds, Development Impact Bonds, and outcome-based contracts.
💸
Equity with Impact Investor ShareholdersWeak structural protection — even aligned investors may sell shares to non-aligned buyers. The Patagonia conversion to a trust (2022) was necessary specifically because equity ownership, even by a founder, does not prevent future sale.

⚠️ Mission Drift Warning Signs

🚨
Gradual Charitable Objects CreepAdding new purposes that slowly expand away from original mission. Common in charities under funding pressure. Charity Commission has seen this pattern repeatedly — requires active trustee oversight.
🚨
Revenue-Driven Programme ChangesShifting to where the funding is rather than where the need is. Affects all structure types. The NCVO Almanac (2024) estimates 30–40% of UK charities have experienced significant programme drift over 10 years.
🚨
Dominant Founder / Dominant Trustee RiskGovernance captured by one person who gradually reorients mission. UK Charity Commission Inquiry Reports (2023) cite this as the #1 governance failure pattern. Independent board members are the primary protection.
🚨
Investor-Driven ConversionPressure to convert from nonprofit to for-profit structure to enable investor exit. Seen in US ed-tech and health-tech sectors (2015–2020). Requires statutory asset lock or significant legal friction to prevent.
